The falls are a natural border between Brazil and Argentina, but 80% of the falls lie on Argentinian side. This meant that we could not leave the area without also visiting the national park on the Argentinian side.

We were picked up at the hotel at 6:40am and headed to the border after making a few more stops at nearby hotels. At the border, we had to wait around 30 minutes for the Argentinian border police to check through our passports. The driver explained that there is a new agreement between Brazil and Argentina that if the tourists are making a day trip to the falls, they do not need a stamp from the Brazilian border police when exiting the country. This made the whole process a lot quick than expected, and we managed to arrive at the park at around 9:00am.

The Brazilian side of the falls was more of a panoramic view compared to the Argentinian side where you could go right above, below and between the waterfall system. There are three main routes that take you around the park. If you complete them you get an abundance of views and in contrast to the Brazilian side you get really close to the waterfalls.

![Waterfall]({{ '/assets/images/260321/waterfall.jpeg' | relative_url }})

![Together]({{ '/assets/images/260321/together.jpeg' | relative_url }})

The first route, Circuita Inferior, took us through the rainforest at a low elevation, giving us dramatic views of the falls from below. We got some company from some very cute coatis on this trail! (Rika thinks that Argentinian coatis are cuter than Brazilian ones.)

![Coati]({{ '/assets/images/260321/coati.jpeg' | relative_url }})

After completing the first route we continued on to the Circuita Superior, which brings you right to on top of the waterfalls. Walking mostly on footpaths we were able to see the falls up close. You're walking over the river which is the source for the falls and it is quite beautiful with clear water and fish swimming around.

![River]({{ '/assets/images/260321/river.jpeg' | relative_url }})

It was approaching 11 am and we headed back to a sort of rest area where we encountered some monkeys! They were trying to get to the food, the opportunistic scoundrels, but we escaped into a cage specifically designed to keep monkeys and coatis out. After hastily finishing our packed lunch we boarded a train for the main  attraction of the day: La Garganta del Diablo!

The train ride took about 15 minutes and brought us to the start of a kilometer-long foot-path over the river. Trekking it took about 20 minutes with a couple of stops for photos. The amount of tourists on this thing was rather remarkable but everything went along relatively smoothly. 

At the end of the foot path a spectacular experience awaited: the biggest waterfall in Iguazu Falls, known as La Garganta del Diablo, or the Devli's Throat. Approaching it you see a cloud of white eater particles arising fron where the bottom of the waterfall and by the time you reach the end of the path you're wet. You're now right above the torrent and the sound is awesome. 1.5 million liters of water crashes down 80 meters, creating an opaque mist totally conceiling the bottom of the fall. The sound and the splashing of water and the neverending flow of water make for a unique experience to say the least.

![Diablo]({{ '/assets/images/260321/diablo.jpeg' | relative_url }})

After wrestling other tourists for the best selfie spot for about 30 minutes we headed back along the foot-path, happy that we'd experienced one of the true naturals wonders of this world.
