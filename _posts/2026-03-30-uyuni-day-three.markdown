Today was the highlight of the trip — we finally got to see the salt flats. We left at 4:30 a.m. to catch the sunrise, and it was absolutely worth it.

When we arrived, part of the flats was covered by a thin layer of rainwater, creating a magical view. During the rainy season, if you’re lucky, you can witness the famous “mirror” effect, where the ground perfectly reflects the sky. This view was the most amazing natural phenomenon we had ever seen.

For the final part of our tour, we visited the train cemetery, an open-air museum filled with old, abandoned trains.

Afterward, we arrived in Uyuni and had our last lunch together as a group.

While researching how to get from Uyuni to La Paz, I had read that it’s best to buy bus tickets last minute because road blockades in Bolivia can be unpredictable. There had been a major one back in March that stranded many travelers in Uyuni. When we got to the bus terminal, I started to feel nervous—online, all the night buses to La Paz were sold out. Luckily, we found one company, Trans Titicaca, that still had seats available. The bus was at 9:30 p.m., so we had the entire day to wait.

A British couple from our tour was also taking a different night bus to La Paz, so we spent the day together playing cards, drinking beer, and having dinner. The time passed surprisingly quickly.

We arrived at the bus station around 8:30 p.m., brushed our teeth, and waited at the lane listed on our ticket. At 9:00 p.m., two Trans Titicaca buses pulled into the terminal. Since our ticket said 9:30 p.m. and listed lane 7, we were confident we were in the right place. However, as time passed, one of the buses from the lane next to us departed at 9:15 p.m.

Kaj with the bus we did not get on
![Bus]({{ '/assets/images/260330/bus.jpeg' | relative_url }})

Then a random backpacker came up to ask which bus we were taking. When we compared tickets, we realized hers said 10:00 p.m.—and it was for the bus that was now waiting in front of us. That’s when it hit us: our bus had already left.

We rushed to the staff checking in passengers for the next bus, and thankfully, they let us board. But once we found our seats, another woman showed up with the same seat numbers. Kaj went to speak to the staff again, and luckily, there were a few empty seats on the lower level, so they let us take those instead.

It was a very stressful end to a long day.

By coincidence, we ended up sitting next to the same girl who had asked us about the bus earlier, and it turned out she was from Japan. We chatted for a while and helped her out later when we had to pay the “bus terminal tax” — something no one had mentioned beforehand.
